    Pattern Drafts

    Understanding fundamentals of reading and designing a pattern draft for loom weaving.

    Similarly to how punchcards inform a knitting machine of how to control the needles, pattern drafts for weaving provide a clear visualisation of what warp threads are being lifted and how they will intersect with the weft yarn. Regardless if you are using a manual loom or a digital/punchcard-based one such as a Jacquard, it defines what happens at each step and a sketch of what your resulting fabric will look like. The diagram below, illustrates the relationship between the four sections that comprise a pattern draft graph.

    Threading: This is showing the pattern that the warp is set up in through the 'headles' and 'harnesses' in the middle section of the loom. Each row signifies a harness and a square/cell is 1 headle. Once you start weaving this cannot be changed anymore.
    Tie-up: This is relevant for floor looms, table looms use different harness mechanisms where no tie-up is needed. Below the main section of the loom that holds the harnesses, the 'lams' (wooden bars underneath the harnesses) need to be attached to the floor treadles. This section is read by the column, from left to right, within which the cells will connect the information in the threading and the treadling pattern to dictate which harnesses are being lifted by each treadle. The tie-up can be changed at any point during weaving.
    Treadling pattern: The treadling sequence provides the weaver the most flexibility and creativity once the loom has been set-up. Experimenting with the relationship of the tie-up and sequence can create varieties of patterns and fabric densities or intricacies.
    Resulting fabric: Following the filled-in cells of the prior 3 sections, the grid design of the final fabric can be sketched out - or otherwise automatically filled in if using a digital design tool, such as Treadl.com for the design on the left.


    This is a fabric sample of the pattern draft above. You can see how the diamond forms in the draft translate to the actual weaving. This specific technique is called waffle weave, the visual effect and malleability that the diamond structurecreates comes as a result of a range of short->long warp and weft floats. In the pattern draft, the long horizontal stripes (weft) and vertical stripes (warp) show the floats, which are otherwise typically something to avoid to prevent instability of the fabric or them catching on your surroundings.
